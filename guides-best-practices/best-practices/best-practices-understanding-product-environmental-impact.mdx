## Why Understanding Product Impact Matters Before You Choose a Tool

Before diving into specific methodologies or software, it helps to understand *why* impact assessment matters in the first place.  
For most organizations—especially SMEs—the goals tend to fall into a few categories:

- **Customer expectations**: Brands need credible sustainability claims to stay competitive.  
- **Retailer requirements**: Many distributors demand environmental data for listings.  
- **Internal decisions**: Knowing which materials or suppliers drive emissions guides product design.  
- **Risk & regulation**: New rules across Europe require clearer environmental communication.  
- **Strategic direction**: Impact data helps prioritize innovation where it matters most.

Understanding these motivations early makes it much easier to choose the right approach—even if you don’t yet know what an LCA is.

## Best Practice 1: Start With a Clear Question, Not a Complex Method

People often search for tools before defining the question they want to answer. But the best sustainability insights start with a simple, practical question:

- “What part of my product has the biggest environmental impact?”  
- “Would switching materials meaningfully reduce our footprint?”  
- “How can we communicate sustainability credibly without greenwashing?”  
- “What decisions should we prioritize for the next product update?”  

Once the question is defined, the appropriate method—and the right tool—becomes much clearer.

## Best Practice 2: Focus on Data You Already Have

You don’t need specialized sustainability data to begin. Most impactful insights come from information you already track:

- Material types and weights  
- Manufacturing locations  
- Key suppliers  
- Packaging formats  
- Transport distances  
- How customers use or dispose of the product  

These inputs create a surprisingly complete picture, especially when supported by AI that fills gaps or suggests proxies where needed.

## Best Practice 3: Look for Patterns, Not Perfect Precision

Many first-time teams assume sustainability analysis needs exact numbers. In reality, early insights are most useful when they help teams:

- Identify hotspots: which materials or steps matter most  
- Compare alternatives: which option significantly lowers impact  
- Spot trade-offs: where improvements shift burdens elsewhere  
- Guide decisions: which design paths are worth exploring  

Patterns—not perfection—drive good sustainability decisions.

Credibility comes from transparency and consistency, not from pretending to have perfect data.

## Best Practice 4: Make It Collaborative From Day One

Sustainability becomes much easier when it's shared across teams.  
Instead of a single sustainability specialist working alone, involve:

- Design teams testing materials  
- Procurement evaluating suppliers  
- Engineers validating processes  
- Leadership assessing business impact  

Early collaboration avoids rework and strengthens the quality of insights.

## Best Practice 6: Communicate Insights in Plain Language

Even if your analysis is technically sound, it won’t influence decisions unless stakeholders understand it.  
Effective communication means:

- Describing impacts with relatable metrics (e.g., CO₂ per product)  
- Explaining assumptions clearly  
- Showing hotspots visually  
- Presenting scenarios with real-world implications  
- Keeping the narrative simple and actionable
