## What Are Carbon Hotspots?

Carbon hotspots are the **materials, processes, or design choices that account for a disproportionately large share of emissions**.

Common examples in construction include:

- Energy-intensive materials such as concrete, steel, and glass  
- Long-distance or fragmented transport routes  
- Short-lived components requiring frequent replacement  
- Poor recovery potential at end-of-life  

<Callout icon="flame" color="#ef4444">
Seemingly small design shifts — like a new facade material or a shorter transport route — can cut embodied impact by 20–40%.
</Callout>

## Why Finding Hotspots Early Matters

Most climate-related risk in construction sits in the *embodied* part of the footprint — long before operations begin.  
Missing these hotspots has serious consequences:

| Missed Insight | Outcome |
|----------------|---------|
| High-impact materials | Overshooting carbon limits or project targets |
| Late design changes | Costly redesign or missed opportunities |
| Manual impact estimates | Inconsistent reporting and slow approvals |
| Non-verified data | Risk to credibility and client trust |

Spotting hotspots early protects both the project timeline and the environmental outcome.

## A Real-World Example: Designing a Lower-Impact Building

A Northern European construction firm reviewed three different façade options during early design.  
Once they mapped the impacts, two hidden factors dominated the footprint:

- The transport distance for imported components  
- The aluminum framing required for a specific design pattern  

These two factors accounted for over half of the embodied impact — far more than the insulation type the team originally planned to optimize.

By sourcing locally and using recycled aluminum, the company reduced its embodied footprint by **more than 30%**, without redesigning the building envelope.

## Turning Hotspot Insights Into Design Strategy

<Tip>Small, early adjustments have the biggest long-term impact.</Tip>

Here’s a simple workflow:

1. **Run early-stage assessments** to catch hotspots before design is locked.  
2. **Collect supplier data** consistently — especially for high-volume materials.  
3. **Model alternatives** to compare material mixes, transport routes, and component lifetimes.  
4. **Share insights visually** with project partners to speed up decisions.  
5. **Refine continuously** as design and procurement evolve.

This process keeps sustainability integrated, not siloed.

## FAQs

**What’s the difference between embodied and operational emissions?**  
Embodied emissions come from materials and manufacturing; operational emissions come from building use. Both matter — but embodied emissions often dominate early-stage decisions.
